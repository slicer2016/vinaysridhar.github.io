Comparison and Validation

A recent interaction left me unsettled — a friend's constant comparisons of fitness stats, phone cameras, and smartwatch features. This discomfort led to an exploration about how we engage with others.

Here's the core insight: Everyone unconsciously sorts life domains into two categories:

(A) Personal: Areas where we compete only with ourselves

(B) Comparative: Areas where we actively compete with others

This sorting explains a lot about social friction. I believe how we categorize these domains stems from two sources:

  1. Insecurity (making something comparative because we need external validation)
  2. Deliberate choice (making something personal because we've decided it serves us better)

Some people (let's call them Maximalist Comparers) make everything a competition. They constantly benchmark against others—fitness, wealth, career, possessions. Others (Internal Benchmarkers) prefer to measure against their own standards.

Tension usually arises when someone imposes their comparative frame onto our personal domains. Surprisingly, this creates discomfort even when we're "winning" the comparison because the act of comparison violates our chosen relationship with that activity.

To minimize personal distress, I have tried to:

  1. Identify which of my 'A' domains others treat as 'B'
  2. Maintaining a "sharp" ego — distinct from a large ego. Keep your identity separate from your activities (see my Principles )
  3. If all else fails, limit interaction with Maximalist Comparers

In contrast, to avoid causing others distress:

  1. Recognize that my 'B' domains (say, career progress) might be others' 'A' domains
  2. Be thoughtful about discussing comparative topics with those who've chosen different life paths
  3. Pay attention to others' discomfort when I discuss a certain topic

This framework explains why certain social interactions drain me while others are energizing. It's not about the topics themselves, but about alignment in how we categorize them.

I think the underlying goal is to develop social awareness and recognize that we all process ourselves and the world differently. Hopefully this makes social interactions less taxing on everyone.
