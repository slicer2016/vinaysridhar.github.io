Cooking

I learned to cook in 2018 after spending two years in the US. I did not have access to good South Indian food in Boston, and learned to cook out of desperation (a fab Sri Lankan restaurant in Cambridge closed shop a few months after I moved). There were many failed attempts - including a week where I kept adding fennel instead of jeera to dishes and wondered why everything tasted like toothpaste.

Reflections on Cooking

  • I basically cook to recreate food I grew up eating. I rarely have the energy to invent new recipes (although one of my greatest hits was a truffle oil chinna vengayam sambar - it was fire!). On most days, my goal is usually fidelity to memory, not creativity. I wonder if this is the difference between trying to be a good craftsman or a good artist.
  • It is pivotal to understand your ingredients, especially vegetables. AFAIK, there isn't a helpful list of these rules in one place so it's usually learned by trial and error. For example, cooking cut okra with water makes it sticky and soggy. The ridges in ridge gourds are bitter - always worth scraping off. Potatoes boiled with baking soda get crispier. This is a huge factor in making food that tastes good.
  • Regional Indian cuisines are defined by their tadkas. What makes Tamil food Tamil is usually a combination of coconut oil, mustard seeds, hing and curry leaves. Masala Lab by Krish Ashok taught me a lot of this, I highly recommend it.
  • I'm still learning to better layer acids. Tamarind, tomatoes and lemon all add sharpness, but have distinctive flavor profiles. Combining them often creates the complexity I remember from childhood.
  • Language shows up in unexpected ways. The word I use for vegetables displays my polyglossic upbringing - if my mother discovered a word growing up in Tamil Nadu, I use the Tamil word. If she started using the vegetable in her 40s in Bangalore, I know the Kannada word. If I encounter the vegetable in restaurants, I end up using the English word. The captions for these pictures illustrate this well - I promise I wasn't trying!
  • Baking requires precision and patience that I don't have. Cooking, on the other hand, forgives your lack of precision as long as you're generally following guidelines. In contrast, I know fabulous bakers who are not great cooks. I wonder if there's something deeper that is predictive of these preferences.
